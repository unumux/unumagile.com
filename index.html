Introduction to Agile

Agile is a group of software development methods in which requirements and solutions evolve through collaboration between self-organizing, cross-functional teams. It promotoes adaptive planning, evolutionary development, early delivery, continuous improvement and encourages rapid and flexible reponse to change. It is a conceptual framework that focuses on frequently delivering small increments of working software.

The Core Team

Agile teams consist of the Product Owner, Scrum Master and Development team. Each team is empowered and self-directed to work together toward a common business goal.

Product Release

Determines when it is appropriate to release the working increments of software to the market. Occurs after each sprint or series of sprints.

Support & Operations

Assists the core team with specialized skills and services when needed but are not fully dedicated to the core team. The operations teams support the software in a production environment.

Individuals and Interactions OVER Processes and tools

To create high-performance teams, agile value individuals and interactions over processes and tools. Practically speaking, all of the agile seek to increase communication and collaboration through frequent inspect-and-adapt cycles.

Working software OVER Comprehensive documentation

Although documentation is still important and has a purpose in software development, demonstrating working software to our business partners will be the primary means of ensuring we understand the problem they are trying to solve.

Customer collaboration OVER Contract negotiation

It is through daily customer (or customer proxy) collaboration that industry data shows that agile projects have more than twice the success rate of traditional projects on average worldwide. Because agile recognize the value of customer engagement, they have created a place on their development teams that is specifically for the customer representative.

Responding to change OVER Following a plan

For teams to create products that will please customers and provide business value, teams must respond to change. Industry data shows that over 60 percent of product or project requirements change during the development of software.

What is Scrum?

Scrum is an Agile Framework of lightweight practices for completing complex projects. Scrum originally was formalized for software development projects, but it works well for any complex, innovative scope of work. The possibilities are endless. The Scrum framework is deceptively simple.

What is Kanban?

A method for managing work with an emphasis on just-in-time delivery while not overloading the team members. In this approach, the process, from definition of a task to its delivery to the customer, is displayed for participants to see and team members pull work from a queue.

What are agile roles?

In pure agile, the roles are Scrum Master, Product Owner, and Development Team (everyone else). This approach works well with small to medium size companies. When using agile on a large scale (scaling agile), the complexity of organizational structures, imbedded cultural behaviors, specialized roles, etc. causes the need for a deeper level of understanding and definition of roles. The Agile Working Team is working through this along with the Operating Model job simplification track and more information will be shared as the agile model and job simplification tracks are rolled out in late 2014/early 2015.

What is the difference between iterative and agile?

We transitioned from a sequencial waterfall approach to software development to a "chunked" iterative approach in 2011 which was a stepping stone to agile. The key hurdle we have really made great progress on with iterative is breaking the work down into smaller, more manageable chunks and getting feedback from the our business partners along the way.

A key focus is to continue to mature in breaking work down and demonstarting pieces of the solution through the life of initiative. In addition, what will we be instituionalizing with agile is some new lightweight practices, like scrum and kanban, new roles, an engagment model for the "horizontal" role such as data and Shared Services partners, BUT MOST IMPORTANT you will find a new mindset in the air. A mindset of flexibility in tools and techniques used; a mindset in autonomy of teams (self-directed teams); and a mindset of business value first and foremost!

Is agile an IT process?

No, the agile values are riddled with the terms collaboration and communication. That means collaboration between business and IT, or better stated between the agile team. The Product Owner role is a member of the business who can prioritize work for the team to ensure high business value is generated quickly and often. This is done through regular (daily) collaboration and feedback. The PO is a key member of the agile core team.
